Any system of \(n\) qubits will have \(2^n\) basis states. As we have seen already, in a one-qubit system, the two basis states are \(\ket{0}\) and \(\ket{1}\text{.}\) In a two-qubit system, the four basis states are \(\ket{00}\text{,}\) \(\ket{01}\text{,}\) \(\ket{10}\text{,}\) and \(\ket{11}\text{.}\) These four basis states each have vector representations, which are found by using the tensor product

The tensor product is only one of many operations on qubits. In Subsection 1.5.4 we described how the inner product could be represented as the product of a vector’s transpose and the vector. Since an \(n\) dimensional vector transpose is \(1\times n\) and the vector is \(n\times1\text{,}\) the resulting product is \(1\times1\text{,}\) which is functionally equivalent to a scalar. If we were to reverse the order of the vector multiplication and multiply a column vector on the left and a row vector on the right, we would multiply an \(n\times1\) vector by an \(1\times m\) vector to produce an \(n\times m\) matrix.
